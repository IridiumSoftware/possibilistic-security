# The Broken Triad: Alcoholism as Metabolic Closure Failure

**Aaron Green**
*April 2026*

---

## What if the craving isn't for the drug?

I stopped drinking alcohol a few months ago. Not through willpower, not through AA, not through medication. I switched to non-alcoholic beer. Same brands, same ritual, same social contexts. Just no ethanol.

The cravings vanished. Not gradually. Not with effort. They just stopped.

This surprised me. I had expected the hard part — the white-knuckling, the counting days, the identity crisis of "I'm a person who doesn't drink now." None of it came. I went to a housewarming party yesterday, drank NA beer and half a coffee, socialized for hours, and had a wonderful time. No pull. No wistfulness. No feeling of deprivation.

That absence of craving is data. And it points to a hypothesis that addiction medicine has the pieces for but hasn't fully assembled: **for at least a subset of people with alcohol problems, the craving is not primarily for ethanol. It is for what beer delivers alongside ethanol — and for the social ritual that beer makes possible.**

---

## The biology: ethanol as a parasite carrying its own antidote

Beer is made with yeast. Yeast, during fermentation, produces significant quantities of B vitamins — especially B1 (thiamine), B6, B9 (folate), and B12. These vitamins are essential for neurotransmitter synthesis: GABA (calming), serotonin (mood stability), dopamine (motivation and reward). Without adequate B vitamins, the brain cannot produce these neurotransmitters reliably. The result is anxiety, irritability, poor sleep, cognitive fog, and a persistent low-grade sense that something is wrong.

Here is the paradox: chronic alcohol consumption *depletes* the very B vitamins that beer delivers.

Ethanol blocks thiamine absorption in the gut, increases urinary excretion of B6 and magnesium, damages the liver's ability to store folate, and inflames the intestinal lining — reducing absorption of everything. Up to 80% of people with alcohol use disorder show thiamine deficiency. The depletion is well-documented and clinically uncontroversial.

So the drinker is caught in a loop:

1. **Drink beer** → B vitamins arrive → neurotransmitter synthesis gets a temporary boost → you feel better.
2. **But ethanol simultaneously** → blocks absorption → depletes stores → neurotransmitter production degrades.
3. **Hours later** → the depletion reasserts → anxiety, irritability, fog return.
4. **The body sends a signal** → "that thing you drank made us feel better, do it again."
5. **You drink again** → back to step 1.

The craving feels like it's for alcohol. But the relief came from the B vitamins. Ethanol gets credit for the fix while being the cause of the break. It is a parasite wearing a mutualism mask — it rides in with the antidote and takes credit for the cure while perpetuating the disease.

---

## The NA beer experiment: strip the parasite, keep the payload

Non-alcoholic beer is still brewed with yeast. It still contains the B-vitamin payload from fermentation. What it does not contain is meaningful ethanol (typically < 0.5% ABV — physiologically negligible).

When I switched to NA beer, I removed the depleter but kept the delivery system. My body continued to receive the micronutrient payload it was asking for. The craving — which I had always assumed was for the buzz — had no reason to persist. The actual request was being answered.

This is a clean natural experiment, limited by being n=1 and uncontrolled. But the prediction is specific and falsifiable: **if the craving is primarily nutritional, then NA beer should eliminate it. If the craving is primarily pharmacological (GABA agonism, dopamine release), NA beer should not satisfy it at all.**

My result: cravings eliminated. The nutritional hypothesis predicted this. The pharmacological hypothesis did not.

## The ritual: why "just take a vitamin pill" won't work

If B-vitamin depletion were the whole story, the solution would be simple: take a supplement. Pop a B-complex pill every morning. Problem solved.

But anyone who has struggled with alcohol knows that a pill does not replace a beer. The deficiency model alone cannot explain why. Something else is happening.

**The ritual is load-bearing.**

Drinking is not just a pharmacological event. It is a behavioral sequence with deep social and sensory structure: the opening, the pouring, the first sip, the glass in your hand, the posture of relaxation it signals to your body and to the people around you. It is the thing you do at 6 PM that says "the work day is over." It is the thing you hold at a party that says "I belong here." It is the thing you share with a friend that says "we are at ease with each other."

Strip the ritual and you create a deprivation signal even if the chemistry is handled. The person taking B-complex pills instead of drinking beer has solved the metabolic problem but created a behavioral void. They are the person at the party holding nothing, doing nothing with their hands, answering the question "can I get you a drink?" with an explanation. The social and sensory closure is broken.

NA beer preserves the entire ritual. You open it, pour it, hold it, sip it, share it. The behavioral loop completes. The social signaling is intact. Nobody asks questions. You are not performing sobriety — you are just drinking beer that happens not to contain the thing that was hurting you.

**Three closures satisfied simultaneously:**
1. **Metabolic:** B vitamins delivered, neurotransmitter synthesis supported.
2. **Behavioral:** Ritual preserved, no deprivation signal, habit loop completed.
3. **Social:** Belonging maintained, social signaling intact, no identity crisis.

No craving has anywhere left to live.

## The social layer: what people actually crave

This goes deeper than ritual. What people are often craving when they crave alcohol is *connection* — the experience of being in community, being at ease, having your guard down, establishing meaning within a social context. Alcohol is the lubricant that modern Western culture has settled on for this purpose. It is the default technology for "being together without armor."

But alcohol is a terrible technology for this. It lowers inhibitions indiscriminately (not selectively), impairs judgment, creates dependency, damages health, and — critically — the connections formed under its influence are neurochemically unreliable. The warmth you felt may have been the GABA talking. The vulnerability you shared may not survive sobriety.

NA beer preserves the *social technology* (the ritual, the context, the signal of "we are at ease") while removing the part that makes the connections unreliable. You are actually present for the connection. The belonging is real because you were actually there for it, not neurochemically approximating presence while absent.

The craving for socialization — for phenomenological self-meaning within a community — is not pathological. It is fundamental. It may be the deepest craving of all, deeper than any substance. What alcohol does is hijack that craving by making itself the gatekeeper of social ease. NA beer removes the gatekeeper without closing the gate.

## Proposed research

The hypothesis is specific enough to test. Here are concrete study designs.

### Trial 1: NA beer vs. B-complex vs. placebo beverage (RCT)

**Design:** Three-arm randomized controlled trial in adults with mild-to-moderate alcohol use disorder who express desire to reduce consumption.

- **Arm A:** Non-alcoholic beer (ad libitum, self-directed timing to match previous drinking patterns)
- **Arm B:** Daily B-complex supplement (B1 100mg, B6 50mg, B9 400mcg, B12 1000mcg) + magnesium glycinate 400mg + placebo beverage matched for appearance/carbonation but without yeast-derived content
- **Arm C:** Placebo beverage only (no B-vitamins, matched for appearance)

**Primary outcome:** Self-reported craving intensity (Penn Alcohol Craving Scale or similar) at 2, 4, 8, and 12 weeks.

**Secondary outcomes:** Alcohol consumption (timeline followback), blood levels of B1/B6/B12/folate/magnesium at baseline and 12 weeks, mood (PHQ-9, GAD-7), sleep quality (PSQI).

**What it tests:** If Arm A (NA beer) outperforms Arm B (B-complex pill), the ritual/behavioral component is load-bearing beyond the nutritional. If both A and B outperform C, the nutritional axis is confirmed. If only A outperforms, the combination of nutrition + ritual is the active ingredient.

**Sample size:** 150-200 participants (50-65 per arm). Standard power calculation for craving-reduction endpoint.

**Duration:** 12 weeks active, 12 weeks follow-up.

### Trial 2: Mechanistic — blood biomarkers before and after NA beer switch

**Design:** Single-arm prospective cohort. 50 regular beer drinkers (>14 drinks/week) who voluntarily switch to NA beer for 8 weeks.

**Measurements at baseline, week 4, week 8:**
- Serum B1, B6, B12, folate, magnesium, homocysteine (marker of B-vitamin functional status)
- Erythrocyte transketolase activity (gold standard for thiamine status)
- Craving intensity
- Alcohol consumption
- Mood and sleep instruments

**What it tests:** Do B-vitamin levels *rise* after ethanol removal even without supplementation (because the NA beer is still delivering and the depleter is gone)? Does craving reduction correlate with B-vitamin normalization?

### Trial 3: Social context — does drinking context matter?

**Design:** Within-subjects crossover. Participants with AUD drink NA beer in two conditions: (a) alone at home, (b) in a social setting (bar, party, dinner). Craving measured before and after each condition.

**What it tests:** If craving reduction is greater in the social condition, the social/ritual component is contributing beyond the nutritional. If equal in both, nutrition alone is sufficient.

### Retrospective analyses on existing data

Several analyses could be done immediately without new trials:

1. **NHANES or UK Biobank:** Cross-reference self-reported alcohol consumption patterns (beer vs. spirits vs. wine) with serum B-vitamin levels and depression/anxiety scores. Prediction: beer drinkers will show a different B-vitamin depletion profile than spirits drinkers, because beer partially resupplies what it depletes.

2. **NA beer market data + public health outcomes:** Countries/regions where NA beer market share has grown significantly (Germany, Middle East, Scandinavia) — is there a detectable signal in alcohol-related hospitalization rates, controlling for overall consumption trends?

3. **Clinical trial databases:** Meta-analysis of existing trials where B-vitamin supplementation was given to AUD patients. Were craving outcomes reported? Were they separated from withdrawal outcomes? The hypothesis predicts B-vitamin repletion should specifically reduce *craving* (the closure-restoration signal), not just prevent neurological complications.

4. **AA/recovery program data:** Surveys of people in recovery — do those who switched to NA beer have different relapse rates than those who abstain from all beer-like beverages? This data may exist in recovery program databases but has likely never been analyzed through this lens.

## What this is and is not

This is a perspective piece proposing a hypothesis, not a clinical finding. The hypothesis — that a meaningful subset of alcohol cravings are nutritional closure-restoration signals rather than purely pharmacological dependence — is:

- **Biologically grounded** in well-established B-vitamin depletion literature
- **Consistent with** existing clinical practice (thiamine repletion is already standard for severe AUD)
- **Supported by** one clean natural experiment (the author's n=1)
- **Falsifiable** through the proposed trials
- **Novel in its framing** — the pieces exist in the literature but have not been assembled into this specific hypothesis with these specific predictions

It does not claim that all alcoholism is nutritional. It does not claim NA beer cures addiction. It claims that the craving has a metabolic component that is under-recognized, that NA beer accidentally addresses it through a combination of nutritional delivery and ritual preservation, and that this is testable.

The strongest version of the claim: **for a subset of people with alcohol problems, the body is not asking for the drug. It is asking for closure — metabolic, behavioral, and social. NA beer provides all three without the toxin. That is why the cravings stop.**

## A personal note

I am not a physician. I am not an addiction researcher. I am a mathematician on medical leave who stopped drinking and noticed something interesting. The observation is mine. The biology is established. The framework for thinking about it came from work on an unrelated problem — identity security — where the same structural pattern (closure, parasitism, obstruction) appeared at a completely different scale.

The best ideas I have had in my life have come from noticing that the same pattern shows up in two places that have no business being related. This is one of those.

If you are struggling with alcohol: try NA beer. It is cheap, legal, available everywhere, and the worst-case outcome is that you drank a non-alcoholic beverage. If it works for you the way it worked for me — if the craving simply stops — then your body was asking for something other than what you thought it was asking for. And that changes everything about how you approach the problem.
